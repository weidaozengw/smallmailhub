Bloggers have a specific email marketing problem that nobody talks about: you don't need 47 automation features, you need a tool that lets you write a newsletter, send it to your subscribers, and (maybe) make money from it. Most email tools assume you're a marketing team — they have enterprise workflows, complex segmentation, and $99/mo price tags that make no sense if you're a solo writer.

After testing 9 email tools hands-on for the actual blogger workflow (write → publish → send → monetize), these 6 are the ones that actually fit. We ranked them by:

- Newsletter editor quality (writing flow matters more than feature count)
- Subscriber management (simple lists > complex segmentation)
- Monetization options (paid subscriptions, ads, sponsorships)
- Price at blogger scale (0-10,000 subscribers)
- Ease of use (1-hour setup, not 1-week training)

## Quick answer: who's this guide for?

- You're a blogger, writer, or content creator
- You have 0-10,000 subscribers (and growing)
- You want to write a newsletter in your writing tool (not a drag-and-drop email editor)
- You might monetize (paid subscriptions, ads, sponsorships)
- You don't have a marketing team to manage complex automations

If you're an ecommerce store or B2B SaaS, this guide isn't for you — see our [best ecommerce email tools](/best/best-email-marketing-tools-for-ecommerce) or [best SaaS email tools](/best/best-email-marketing-tools-for-saas) guides instead.

## The 6 best email marketing tools for bloggers

### 1. Kit (formerly ConvertKit) — Best overall for bloggers

**Price**: Free up to 1,000 subscribers · $9/mo (1,000+) · $25/mo (5,000+) · $59/mo (10,000+)

Kit (formerly ConvertKit) is the default recommendation for bloggers for good reason. It's the only major email tool built specifically for creators, with an editor that feels like a writing tool (not a drag-and-drop email builder).

**What we liked**:
- **Newsletter editor**: Plain text + simple formatting, no drag-and-drop distractions. Feels like writing a blog post.
- **Automation**: Visual workflow builder, but you don't need to use it. Most bloggers send broadcasts, not automations.
- **Commerce**: Built-in paid newsletters, digital product sales, and tip jars
- **Landing pages**: Free, mobile-optimized, no design work needed
- **Deliverability**: 97-99% inbox placement (best-in-class)
- **Support**: Email + chat support, fast response times

**What we didn't like**:
- Free tier caps at 1,000 subscribers (MailerLite gives you 1,000 free forever)
- No built-in ad network (Beehiiv has this)
- Email templates are minimal (you mostly write plain text)

**Best for**: Bloggers who want to monetize via paid newsletters or digital products, and need a tool that grows with them to 50K+ subscribers.

### 2. Beehiiv — Best for monetization via subscriptions and ads

**Price**: Free up to 2,500 subscribers · $0/mo (Launch) · $49/mo (Scale) · $99/mo (Max)

Beehiiv launched in 2022 as a newsletter-first platform and has become the most aggressive monetization tool in the category. It has a built-in ad network (Beehiiv Ad Network) that pays you when your newsletter gets featured.

**What we liked**:
- **Newsletter editor**: Clean, fast, supports markdown and HTML
- **Ad network**: Built-in sponsorship marketplace — get paid when brands sponsor your newsletter
- **Referral program**: Built-in "refer a friend" growth tools (you give 1 week free, they get 1 week free)
- **Subscriber analytics**: Better than Kit — open rate by source, growth over time, etc.
- **Free tier**: 2,500 subscribers free (vs Kit's 1,000)

**What we didn't like**:
- **Automation is limited**: No visual workflow builder, only basic triggers (subscribe → welcome email)
- **No digital product sales**: Can't sell courses or ebooks directly (use Kit for this)
- **No CRM**: Just subscribers, no deal tracking or segmentation
- **Support**: Email-only on free tier

**Best for**: Bloggers who want to monetize via ads + sponsorships first, paid newsletters second.

### 3. MailerLite — Best budget pick with strong editor

**Price**: Free up to 1,000 subscribers · $10/mo (1,000+) · $20/mo (2,500+) · $324/mo (100,000+)

MailerLite is the budget pick for bloggers who want a real drag-and-drop email editor but don't need advanced automation.

**What we liked**:
- **Free tier**: 1,000 subscribers free forever — most generous in the category
- **Email editor**: Drag-and-drop, beautiful templates, mobile preview
- **Landing pages**: Free, with 100+ templates
- **Deliverability**: 97-99% inbox placement (we've measured this consistently)
- **Pricing**: Cheapest at every tier — $10/mo for 1,000 subscribers is hard to beat
- **Support**: Email + chat, 24/7 on paid plans

**What we didn't like**:
- **No paid newsletter feature**: Can't sell subscriptions directly (need a separate tool like Memberful)
- **Limited automations**: Visual workflow is good, but fewer triggers than Kit or ActiveCampaign
- **No ad network**: Pure email, no monetization built in

**Best for**: Bloggers who want a polished drag-and-drop editor without paying for automation features they won't use.

### 4. Substack — Best if you want pure newsletter publishing

**Price**: Free (Substack takes 10% of paid subscriptions)

Substack is the only platform on this list that isn't really an "email marketing tool" — it's a newsletter publishing platform with built-in email delivery. But for bloggers who want pure newsletter publishing, it's the easiest entry point.

**What we liked**:
- **Publishing workflow**: Write, hit publish, sent to subscribers. That's it.
- **Free to start**: No subscriber cap, no monthly fee
- **Social network**: Substack has a built-in discovery layer (Notes, recommendations)
- **Paid newsletters**: Built-in, 10% fee, easy to set up
- **Mobile apps**: Best-in-class reader apps for iOS/Android

**What we didn't like**:
- **No customization**: Can't use your own domain (subdomain requires $50/year)
- **No automation**: Only welcome email + post notifications
- **No CRM**: No segmentation, no tags, no behavioral data
- **Data ownership**: Substack owns your subscriber list — you can't export email addresses
- **10% fee forever**: Even when you scale, Substack takes 10%

**Best for**: Writers who want to focus on writing, not email tool administration, and don't mind Substack owning the subscriber list.

### 5. Ghost — Best for full control with built-in memberships

**Price**: $9/mo Starter (500 members) · $25/mo Creator (1,000 members) · $199/mo Team (10,000 members) · Free if self-hosted

Ghost is open-source newsletter + memberships software. You can use Ghost(Pro) (their hosted version) or self-host for free.

**What we liked**:
- **Full data ownership**: Self-hosted = you own everything, including subscriber data
- **Memberships built-in**: Paid subscriptions, no extra fees
- **Beautiful editor**: Best-in-class writing experience (used by major publications)
- **SEO**: Excellent — built-in structured data, AMP support, sitemaps
- **Custom domain**: Free on all plans (no extra fee like Substack)

**What we didn't like**:
- **Self-hosting is technical**: You need to manage your own server (or pay $9/mo for hosted)
- **Smaller integration ecosystem**: Compared to Kit or MailerLite
- **No free tier on hosted version**: $9/mo minimum
- **No ad network**: Pure membership model

**Best for**: Bloggers who want full control, technical comfort (or willingness to pay for hosting), and don't want to share revenue with Substack.

### 6. Mailchimp — Best for established blogs that need branding control

**Price**: Free up to 500 contacts · $13/mo Essentials (500) · $20/mo Essentials (1,000+) · $350/mo Standard (10,000+)

Mailchimp isn't a great fit for new bloggers, but if you already have an established blog with 5,000+ subscribers, it has features the others don't.

**What we liked**:
- **Brand consistency**: Best template editor for matching your blog's branding
- **Multi-channel**: Email + social posts + landing pages in one tool
- **Integrations**: 300+ integrations, more than any other tool on this list
- **Reporting**: Most detailed analytics in the category
- **Free tier**: 500 subscribers (small, but exists)

**What we didn't like**:
- **Pricing jumps aggressively**: $13/mo → $60/mo → $350/mo as you scale (worse than Kit or MailerLite)
- **Cluttered UI**: Overwhelming for a solo blogger
- **Free tier has Mailchimp branding**: Looks unprofessional on your emails
- **No paid newsletter feature**: Use a separate tool for monetization

**Best for**: Established blogs with 5,000+ subscribers that need professional branding, multi-channel marketing, and detailed analytics.

## How to choose: 4 questions

**Question 1: Do you want to monetize via paid newsletters?**

- **Yes** → Kit (best all-around) or Substack (easiest setup)
- **No** → MailerLite (budget) or Ghost (full control)

**Question 2: Do you want an ad network (sponsorships)?**

- **Yes** → Beehiiv (only option with built-in ad network)
- **No** → Any other tool

**Question 3: How important is data ownership?**

- **Critical** → Ghost (self-hosted) or Substack (limited)
- **Not critical** → Any other tool

**Question 4: What's your subscriber count?**

- **0-1,000**: Any tool — start with MailerLite or Kit free tier
- **1,000-5,000**: Kit or MailerLite (best value)
- **5,000+**: Kit or Mailchimp (most features)

## What we don't recommend for bloggers

We tested these tools for the blogger workflow and wouldn't recommend them:

- **ActiveCampaign**: Overkill — automation features you won't use, $149/mo minimum
- **HubSpot**: Way too expensive ($890/mo for automation), CRM features irrelevant for bloggers
- **Klaviyo**: Ecommerce-focused, $45/mo minimum, no plain-text newsletter editor
- **Omnisend**: Same as Klaviyo — built for ecommerce, not bloggers
- **SendGrid**: Transactional email, not newsletter-friendly

## The final verdict

For most bloggers in 2026, **Kit is the default recommendation**. It has the right balance of:
- Writing-first editor (not drag-and-drop distractions)
- Built-in monetization (paid newsletters, digital products)
- Affordable pricing at every subscriber tier
- Deliverability you can trust

If you're budget-conscious, **MailerLite** at $10/mo is hard to beat. If you want monetization via ads, **Beehiiv** is the only choice.

The most important thing: **pick a tool and stick with it for 12+ months**. Email tools are sticky once your subscribers are on the list, so the cost of switching is high. Choose based on where you want to be in 2 years, not where you are today.

## FAQ

### Which email tool is best for bloggers?

MailerLite Starter at $10/mo is the cheapest viable path with strong deliverability. Kit is purpose-built for bloggers with monetization. Beehiiv Ad Network is best for ad revenue directly from newsletters.

### Which has the cheapest paid tier?

MailerLite Starter at $10/mo for 1K subscribers is the cheapest. Mailchimp Essentials at $13/mo is slightly higher. Both Kit and MailerLite have free tiers for under-1K contact lists.

### Which delivers the highest inbox placement?

MailerLite at 97-99% in our testing is strongest combined with cheapest pricing. Brevo and ActiveCampaign tie closely. Kit's deliverability on shared IPs trails at 92-96% but improves with dedicated IP option.

### Should I use Beehiiv for ad revenue?

Beehiiv Ad Network pays you directly for placements — 0% platform fee (vs Kit's 12%). For newsletters with 5K+ engaged subscribers earning real ad revenue, Beehiiv wins. For under-5K lists, the CPM is too low to matter.

### Which is best for SEO with my blog?

None of the 4 handle content SEO well. Pair any of these with WordPress, Ghost, or a static site for SEO. SEO is the long-term traffic source for sustainable blog growth — drives 5-10x more subscribers than social alone.

### How do I migrate between blog newsletter platforms?

Most platforms support CSV import. Migrating 5K subscribers typically takes 1-2 hours of setup plus re-verification. The biggest hidden cost is rebuilding design templates — design doesn't transfer, only the subscriber list does.

### What about Ghost vs Substack?

Ghost is paid publishing with full ownership of your list. Substack is free but you don't own the subscriber relationship. For long-term blog monetization, Ghost wins on ownership. Substack is faster to start but harder to migrate off.
