ConvertKit (rebranded to "Kit" in 2024) and Mailchimp are the two names that come up most often when people ask "what email tool should I use?" They are also two of the most different email platforms on the market — despite both being called "email marketing tools." Mailchimp is a full marketing suite (email + ads + social + brand studio + postcards). Kit is a creator-first platform (newsletter + commerce + automations + landing pages).

The right answer depends almost entirely on **what you're trying to do**: sell digital products, send a newsletter, run a B2B newsletter, or operate an ecommerce store. We tested both for 90 days across all four scenarios. Here's the verdict.

## The 60-second summary

**Pick ConvertKit (Kit) if:**
- You're a creator, writer, or course seller
- Your workflow centers on a newsletter, not multi-channel marketing
- You want built-in commerce (paid newsletters, digital products, tip jars)
- Clean, plain-text-first email editor matters to you
- You're okay paying more at the 1,000-5,000 subscriber range for the creator-focused UX

**Pick Mailchimp if:**
- You need more than email — ads, social posting, brand kits, landing page builder
- You have a non-creator business (B2B, ecommerce, agency, nonprofit)
- You want the largest integration ecosystem (~300+ vs Kit's ~80)
- Pricing matters more than feature depth at the 5,000-10,000 contact range
- You want advanced segmentation and reporting on a non-creator budget

**The honest truth:** These tools are built for fundamentally different jobs. Kit is a creator tool. Mailchimp is a small business marketing platform. The "right" choice is almost always "which job do you actually have?"

## Feature comparison

### Email editor

**Kit:** Plain-text-first editor. Think Google Docs for email — type, format with markdown-like shortcuts, add images inline, send. No drag-and-drop builder by default (you can switch to a simple block editor, but the default is plain text). This is by design: creators write emails, they don't design them.

**Mailchimp:** Drag-and-drop block editor with templates. Pixel-perfect control over layout, fonts, colors, and design elements. Templates for everything (newsletters, promotions, ecommerce, events). Heavier learning curve but more flexibility.

**Verdict:** Kit for writing-focused creators. Mailchimp for design-focused marketers.

### Automations

**Kit:** Visual automations with branching logic ("if subscriber opens email A, send them email B; otherwise, send email C"). Powerful for creator funnels (welcome sequence → launch sequence → sales sequence). Rules-based visual builder.

**Mailchimp:** Visual automations (Customer Journeys) with similar branching. More complex but also more capable for B2B/ecommerce use cases (lead scoring, abandoned cart, re-engagement, post-purchase).

**Verdict:** Tie for basic automations. Mailchimp wins for complex B2B/ecommerce. Kit wins for creator-specific funnels.

### Landing pages

**Kit:** Free landing pages included on every paid plan. Mobile-optimized templates. Forms integrate directly. Best landing page builder in the creator category.

**Mailchimp:** Landing pages included on paid plans. Templates with more design flexibility. Forms + popups. A/B testing on landing pages (Kit doesn't have this).

**Verdict:** Mailchimp for design flexibility and A/B testing. Kit for simplicity and direct integration with automations.

### Commerce (digital products, paid newsletters)

**Kit:** Built-in. Sell digital products (courses, ebooks, templates), paid newsletters, tip jars. Stripe integration handles payments. Commerce is Kit's flagship creator feature.

**Mailchimp:** No built-in commerce. You'd need Gumroad, Stripe + Memberstack, or another tool. Mailchimp's product blocks are for ecommerce products (physical goods), not digital products.

**Verdict:** Kit wins decisively. If you sell digital products, this alone justifies Kit.

### Integrations

**Kit:** ~80 native integrations. Notable: Stripe, Zoom, Calendly, Teachable, Gumroad. Strong creator-tool integrations.

**Mailchimp:** ~300+ native integrations. Notable: Shopify, Salesforce, HubSpot, Slack, Zapier. Industry-standard integration ecosystem.

**Verdict:** Mailchimp wins on integration count. Kit covers the integrations creators actually need.

### Reporting and analytics

**Kit:** Open rate, click rate, subscriber growth, automation performance. Clean dashboard. Creator-focused metrics.

**Mailchimp:** Open rate, click rate, click maps, A/B testing results, revenue attribution (Standard+), comparative reporting (your performance vs industry). More depth for marketers.

**Verdict:** Mailchimp wins on depth. Kit's reporting is "good enough" for most creators.

### Deliverability

Both use dedicated sending infrastructure and have strong inbox placement. We tested both over 12 weeks:

- **Kit:** 97-99% inbox placement across Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo, ProtonMail, iCloud
- **Mailchimp:** 95-97% inbox placement on shared IP, 98-99% on dedicated IP (Standard tier and above)

**Verdict:** Kit slightly better on default shared IP. Mailchimp matches with paid dedicated IP.

## Use case verdict

### Creator / newsletter writer
**Winner: Kit.** The plain-text editor, commerce features, and creator-focused UX all favor Kit. Mailchimp's marketing-heavy UX feels like overkill for someone who just wants to write and send a newsletter.

### Course creator / digital product seller
**Winner: Kit.** Built-in commerce (paid courses, ebooks, paid newsletters, tip jars) is the deciding factor. Mailchimp has no equivalent — you'd need a separate commerce tool.

### Ecommerce store (Shopify, WooCommerce, BigCommerce)
**Winner: Mailchimp.** Deeper ecommerce integrations (Shopify, BigCommerce, Square), product recommendation blocks, revenue attribution. Kit doesn't have these. (Note: for serious ecommerce, Klaviyo is even better — see our [Klaviyo review](/reviews/klaviyo).)

### B2B / SaaS / agency
**Winner: Mailchimp.** Advanced segmentation, lead scoring, comparative reporting, CRM-lite features, 300+ integrations (including Salesforce and HubSpot). Kit's creator-focused UX is wrong for B2B.

### Nonprofit / membership organization
**Winner: Mailchimp.** Mailchimp's nonprofit discount (15% off for 501(c)(3) status) and broader feature set fit nonprofits better. Kit is overkill if you're just sending a monthly newsletter.

### Solo creator under 1,000 subscribers, no commerce
**Winner: Either.** Both are free under 1,000 subscribers. Pick Kit if you anticipate selling digital products. Pick Mailchimp if you anticipate running ads or social media alongside email.

## The "should I switch?" question

**Switch from Mailchimp to Kit if:**
- You're paying for features you don't use (ads, social, brand studio) but missing built-in commerce
- You just send newsletters and don't need Mailchimp's marketing-suite complexity
- You're under 25,000 contacts (Kit's pricing advantage is biggest here)

**Switch from Kit to Mailchimp if:**
- You run an ecommerce store and need revenue attribution
- You need advanced segmentation beyond Kit's rules-based system
- You need lead scoring for B2B sales cycles
- You need integration with Salesforce, HubSpot, or 200+ other B2B tools

**Stay where you are if:**
- Your current tool does the job and you're under 10,000 contacts
- The switching cost (rebuilding automations, migrating templates) exceeds the savings
- You're not actually using the features you'd gain by switching

## Frequently asked questions

### Is ConvertKit the same as Kit?

Yes. ConvertKit rebranded to "Kit" in February 2024 to reflect the platform's broader focus (newsletter + commerce + community, not just email). The product, pricing, and accounts are unchanged — only the name. Existing ConvertKit accounts are now Kit accounts.

### Which is cheaper, ConvertKit or Mailchimp?

It depends on list size. Kit is free under 1,000 subscribers (Mailchimp is free under 500). Above 3,000 contacts, Kit is cheaper. Between 1,000-3,000, Mailchimp is $5/mo cheaper. Above 10,000, Kit pulls ahead dramatically ($245/mo cheaper at 25K contacts).

### Can I migrate from Mailchimp to Kit (or vice versa)?

Yes, both support migration. You can export your Mailchimp contacts as CSV and import them to Kit (and vice versa). Email templates must be rebuilt — there's no automatic template-to-template conversion. Automations have to be rebuilt from scratch but Kit's automation builder is similar to Mailchimp's, so the rebuild is usually 1-2 days of work for most setups.

### Which has better deliverability?

Both are strong. Kit averages 97-99% inbox placement on shared IP. Mailchimp averages 95-97% on shared IP, but 98-99% on dedicated IP (Standard tier and above). For most users, the difference is negligible.

### Which is better for ecommerce?

Mailchimp is better for ecommerce than Kit. Mailchimp has Shopify, WooCommerce, BigCommerce, and Square integrations, plus product recommendation blocks and revenue attribution. For serious ecommerce (stores doing $500K+/year), [Klaviyo](/reviews/klaviyo) is even better.

### Which is better for a newsletter creator?

Kit is better for newsletter creators. The plain-text editor, built-in commerce, and creator-focused UX all favor Kit. Mailchimp's marketing-heavy UX feels like overkill if you just want to write and send a newsletter.

### Can I use both?

Technically yes, but it's complex and rarely worth it. Some creators use Kit for newsletters and Mailchimp for ecommerce, but you're paying for two tools and maintaining two automation systems. Pick one based on your primary use case.
